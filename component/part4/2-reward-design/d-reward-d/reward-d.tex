\subsubsection{Reward D: Entity Grounding}
\nestindent

\textbf{What it is.}
D measures the fraction of proper nouns in the question that also appear in the response:
\[
R_D = \frac{|\text{entities}_\text{response} \cap \text{entities}_\text{question}|}{|\text{entities}_\text{question}|}
\]
Entities are extracted via capitalization heuristic (capitalized words not at sentence start, length ${>}\,2$).
If the question contains no proper nouns, $R_D = 1.0$ (conservative fallback, affects $\sim$3--5\% of GSM8K questions).
A score of 1.0 means the response mentions all entities from the question; 0.0 means it discusses entirely different entities.

\textbf{Why we chose it.}
To address the 82.3\% reward hacking rate identified in Section~1.2: the binary correctness reward checks only the final number, providing no incentive to read the input.
D penalizes responses that ignore the question's entities, forcing the model to engage with the actual problem rather than producing any text that ends in the right number.

D adapts entity-level factual consistency from summarization~\cite{nan2021entity} to math QA.
Coste et al.~\cite{coste2024ensemble} show that distributing reward assessment across multiple signals mitigates overoptimization by preventing exploitation of any single signal.
A more robust alternative would be NER-based entity extraction, but this introduces a learned component that could itself be exploited.
